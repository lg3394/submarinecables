Tracking 15 Years of Submarine Cable Expansion

Ninety-nine percent of global data flows beneath the ocean – here’s how those subsea connections are growing.

By Lucia de la Torre

In an era defined by instantaneous global communication, few people pause to consider the physical infrastructure that makes it possible. While satellites orbit overhead, capturing headlines and imagination, it is the vast network of fiber optic cables silently stretching across ocean floors that truly connects our world – carrying over 99% of all international data traffic.

An exclusive analysis of international submarine cable development reveals a digital arms race unfolding in the depths of our oceans, with geopolitical implications that extend far beyond simple connectivity metrics. As nations and tech giants pour billions into expanding this critical infrastructure, clear patterns of influence and strategic positioning are emerging.

The dramatic rise in submarine cable construction accelerated significantly from 2015 onward, coinciding with the global expansion of cloud computing services and increasing data demands.

Submarine cables are the invisible backbone of the internet, carrying vast amounts of global data traffic across continents. In recent years, especially with the rise of AI-powered technologies, the demand for data collection has fueled the expansion of submarine cable infrastructure.

Most of these undersea cables are owned by individual private companies or consortiums of them, including major tech firms like Google, Facebook (Meta), Amazon, and telecommunications giants. Governments also play a role, often in partnership with private firms, which raises questions about data governance, digital sovereignty, control, and cybersecurity threats.

Trans-Pacific routes lead with the longest submarine cable infrastructure, followed by Trans-Atlantic and Europe-Asia-Africa corridors.

The data reveals striking regional disparities in connectivity. While the Trans-Pacific region boasts the greatest cable length at 320,000 kilometers, it's actually the Intra-Asia region that contains the highest number of individual cable systems. This concentration reflects both Asia's population density and its strategic importance in global digital infrastructure.

Industry experts point to a concerning vulnerability: despite their critical importance, these cables remain surprisingly vulnerable to both accidental damage and deliberate sabotage. A 2024 report from the International Cable Protection Committee documented over 150 incidents affecting submarine cables in the past year alone – a 35% increase from 2023.

Analysts suggest that future cable projects will increasingly be evaluated not just on their commercial merits, but on their strategic value to national interests. "The undersea cable network is transitioning from pure telecommunications infrastructure to a component of national security," notes Dr. Elena Markov, senior fellow at the Digital Infrastructure Policy Institute. "We're witnessing a fundamental shift in how these projects are financed, routed, and secured."
